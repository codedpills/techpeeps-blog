# Go for Gold: Raymond's Winding Road from Pilot Dreams to Data Consulting

"It's been hard," Raymond told me, somewhere in the middle of our conversation, with the kind of honesty you don't always get from someone listing the impressive things on their CV. "I wouldn't want anyone to be listening to this and think I'm superhuman or even think that I have it all covered."

That's the thread I kept tugging on. Because on paper, Raymond is exactly the kind of guest who could make a person feel small. A trained data scientist working as a data consultant in the UK. A musician. A polyglot who did a master's in *both* French and English. A podcast host. And yet the most striking thing he kept returning to wasn't the wins; it was how often he struggled, and how he made peace with that.

Most of the profiles I've featured here are software engineers in a fairly tidy line of work. Raymond's path is anything but tidy, and that's exactly why I wanted to paint the full picture.

## A pilot who got talked into tech

Let's rewind, because you can't understand where Raymond landed without seeing where little Raymond was pointed.

He grew up in Ghana, went through junior high and on to senior high at Mfantsipim, where, as he put it almost reluctantly, "I had the chance to be the domestic head prefect." Before any of that, though, there was French. His dad travelled a lot and kept coming home with language CDs, and somewhere in there a "crazy interest for the French language" took hold. He went to Alliance Française for three months and got good enough that, by his account, his professor was "very excited by the way I was writing my essays."

But the dream wasn't language. The dream was the cockpit. "I wanted to be a pilot," he said, and then, tellingly, "Some parts of me still want to be a pilot."

It was his father, who works in the aviation industry, who nudged him sideways. Planes were becoming computerized, the reasoning went, so a tech degree could be a safety net. "At least have a degree that you can fall on so that in case anything happens, you can still, you know, have a career."

So Raymond read IT at the University of Ghana, not out of passion, but as a "gateway" to flying. And he's refreshingly blunt about how that went. "I did really struggle during my time in University of Ghana," he admitted, wondering aloud whether more enthusiasm and more information going in might have made him "a better developer than I am today."

### The final-year project that finally clicked

For a course that felt "very theoretical," with classes coding in Visual Basic or Java that "just felt so far away," the turning point was his final-year project: a traffic navigation system for Ghana, built in Android Studio, back when Google had no traffic data for the country. He taught himself the Google API, played with Dijkstra's algorithms, and ran around his own house just to test whether the app could detect speed.

It wasn't pretty. "I'm not a UI/UX designer," he laughed. But it was the first time the work "really reflected the skills that I had." His sister, he says, still can't quite believe he stuck around in tech given how much he wrestled with it.

## The detour that became the door

Here's where the story does something I love: the thing that didn't work out opened the thing that did.

The airline where Raymond had interned, his clearest path toward piloting, collapsed right as he was getting ready. Meanwhile, his department, impressed by his projects, wanted him as a teaching assistant. He's careful to note that he didn't fit the usual "really brainy" first-class profile that role implies, but after consulting his parents, he took it.

And that decision put him in the orbit of a man he still talks about: Mr. Anand, who was doing a professional doctorate in data science. "He mentioned that whole aspect of data science," Raymond recalled, "and I was like, what is this? Because I was looking for a way out."

That curiosity became a master's. Which became a career. Which, to him, still feels "very surreal."

## What a data analyst (and a data scientist) actually does

For readers who've heard the titles thrown around without much definition, Raymond drew the line cleanly. A data analyst, he explained, is "looking out for, you know, some insights in the data," answering questions stakeholders already have, presenting things "in an understandable format." A data scientist works on the same data but reaches forward: "this time they want to be able to make some predictions."

His examples were grounded: predicting house prices in an area over time, weighing factors like GDP or policy. "A data analyst is looking forward to gain some firsthand insights into their data," he summed up, "whilst a data scientist's work will be more about predicting the next event based on the data they currently have."

## Surviving France

If there's a chapter that earns the "go for gold" mantra, it's the master's.

Raymond didn't even land in the data programme at first; he was admitted to IoT, the very networking-flavoured world that gave him "headache" with Cisco Packet Tracer back in Ghana. His parents encouraged him to take it as a foot in the door and pivot later. So off he went to Brest, on France's western Atlantic coast, the "only Ghanaian in my school," studying in both French and English, in a rigorous system that had him spending "my entire day indoors."

To switch into data science, he had to perform. He broke his back in the library, passed the gatekeeping courses, and then, in a cruel twist, found that a couple of grades he thought he'd passed actually failed, leaving him resitting two modules *while* starting the data science programme, *in French*. There were 3D-modelling projects on bio-friendly bassoon reeds, signal processing, Fourier transforms, and a class so intense one student dropped out entirely.

The experience "was such a challenging one," he said, "but it really built me in so many ways and I'm really grateful for that." He's not romanticizing it. He's saying both things are true at once: it was brutal, and it made him.

## Wearing every hat at BigSpark

Raymond gently corrected me on one thing, and I'll honour it here. He's a *trained* data scientist, but his day job is as a **data consultant** at BigSpark, mostly serving financial-services clients.

He got in through a recommendation (a friend vouched for him), then ran the gauntlet: an informal chat, a take-home data engineering challenge using Zeppelin, a presentation with code pushed to GitHub, and a final human conversation about whether he leaned technical or managerial. They liked him enough to sponsor his visa from France to the UK, something he credits, in his words, to God: "I really give all the credit to God."

And the consulting reality? "You are wearing so many hats." He's honest that he hasn't done "a lot of data science work" since joining; instead it's been "more data engineering, more software engineering, and DevOps." A custom Neo4j connector for StreamSets in Java. A Python package giving a bank "one point of entry" into its data lake (still in use, he notes proudly). API work with GraphQL and Spring Boot. Now DevOps and GitLab. Plus a side project on a transpiler using ASTs.

He's aware of how it lands. "Some of my friends will probably be watching this video and be like, oh my God, wow, Raymond sounds very brainy." But he's quick to deflate the myth, which is exactly what makes him easy to root for.

## "Skills trump passion," and being patient with yourself

This is where Raymond and I really connected, because I'm at that stage of wondering about the *next* thing myself. He's reading *So Good They Can't Ignore You* by Cal Newport, and it's reframed how he thinks: "building your career is not destination is a journey."

He had a line about all of us that I won't soon forget: "Who really goes to university and says their passion is writing programs?" And then, honestly: "The reason why we are doing this programming thing is because we need to pay our bills." It's not anti-passion; it's a case for building skills so good that people *want* to pay you for them, and leaving yourself free to evolve.

That same gentleness extends to how he treats himself. He doesn't have "a clear path." Projects sit "in the skeleton state" while life pulls him in different directions. And he's learning to "control the things that I can control" and accept the rest.

## The podcast, and the bigger point about tech

Outside work, Raymond runs **[The Tech Surf Podcast](https://open.spotify.com/show/4ryC5dBocN8VHBH4jq8Me3?si=0d7b28f4412348de)**, "my baby," as he calls it, built on a conviction that "tech is not the final destination. Tech is enabling us solve some problem." He platforms people who've used tech to pivot somewhere surprising: a friend who became a chef (Chef Keeks), a classmate who became a travel experience designer, pharmacists turned product managers. "It's my little light that I'm trying to shine in the tech space."

You can find it on YouTube, Spotify, and Apple Podcasts.

## Stay resilient, and allow yourself to breathe

I asked Raymond to leave us with something, and he gave us plenty, but the heart of it was this: "be patient with yourself."

"Celebrate the small wins," he urged. "Take inventory of how far you've come." Get a mentor, or *become* one, because mentoring reveals your own gaps. Don't fear new environments. And, in a moment that felt important coming from a man in tech, to other people in tech, "if you need to cry sometimes, don't be afraid to cry. It's very normal."

His personal mantra, the one stitched through every chapter of this story: "stay resilient and to go for gold."

For someone who didn't see himself in tech, who struggled through university, who broke his back in a French library and still calls himself an ordinary developer, Raymond has built something quietly remarkable, and shared it with disarming honesty. The world is better for the light he's trying to shine.

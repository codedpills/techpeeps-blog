There is a story Joshua Uyie tells that made me wince the moment I heard it. He was helping interview a candidate for an engineering role. The candidate joined the call, fought through some technical hiccups, and then, before anything else could happen, asked to cancel the interview. His reasoning? He had seen the role was a "more senior position and he doesn't think that he can uh, take that role."

Joshua's reaction was blunt: "I was shocked, very shocked." As he put it, "your, your CV was there, they clearly saw your CV, and you were still invited for the interview." Someone had already looked at this person's résumé and decided he was worth a conversation. And he talked himself out of the room before the room had a chance to say no.

"Don't self-reject," Joshua said. That single line ended up being the spine of our whole conversation.

## The Visual Basic origin story (and a nickname that never came true)

Before we get to the CV advice and the job-hunt tactics, I wanted to rewind, because Joshua did not grow up dreaming in code. Quite the opposite.

"A lot of people were like, he's going to be a doctor or something like that," he told me. The label stuck so hard that people "nicknamed me Dr. Joshua when I was like still a child." Medicine was the plan, right up until the end of secondary school, when he noticed something about himself: he understood how the electronics around him worked. There was even a night the family got a new television, too late to call anyone to set it up, and young Joshua wired it in himself.

That was the nudge. He aimed for computer engineering, missed the school and the course he wanted, and landed on computer science at a different university as the alternative. "Well, it's not engineering, but anyways, it's still computer," he remembered thinking. He even had a chance to switch back later and chose to stay.

His real spark came from Visual Basic, the same language a lot of us cut our teeth on. I told him I remembered hooking up a little UI in my school days and feeling like a wizard, and he lit up at the memory of his first login system: "it somehow worked and I was just excited that I was able to make a new window appear." Eight years and several stacks later, that same intuitive pull is still there. Sometimes, he said, he does not even need to study a piece of code. "Intuitively, I could say, okay, this is how it is supposed to work."

## Why he can code in "any programming language you give to me"

Joshua now works as a full-stack developer at Big Viking Games in Canada, and he has moved between multiple languages over the years. When I asked how anyone switches stacks that fluidly, expecting some secret trick, his answer was almost stubbornly unglamorous.

"One thing that is core to software development is understanding the fundamentals," he said. He sees people trying to skip them constantly. His example landed hard: someone once asked him to help a friend learn React, and Joshua's first question was whether the person even knew JavaScript. "Like, no, but there's a job that person was trying to get."

Skip the base, he warned, and "what you just have is, uh, you just built a tower on that particular place that you are." Build the fundamentals, though, and they transfer anywhere. "I do tell people that I can code in any programming language you give to me," he said, with the honest caveat that he means simple programs and algorithms first: "I just need to understand the syntax, how to run the program, and, and I'm good."

He is realistic about how volatile the field is. React introduced Hooks, he noted, and suddenly everyone had to learn them: "We didn't ask for it, but they just had to add it." The lesson is flexibility. If a new job hands you a stack you have never touched, "Would you resign?"

## For the beginner: study, then find your people

Readers just starting out will want the map, so I pushed him on it. Joshua was clear that there are no shortcuts around the effort itself. "You can't do without reading and studying to be a software engineer. It's practically impossible."

But he made the on-ramp gentle. Start with the basics, he said, even something as elementary as what a computer is, or "the difference between input and output devices." The beautiful part, in his words: "Most of the resources you would need are available to you for free."

From basics, move to concepts, understanding *why* something like object-oriented programming exists before drowning in implementation. He admitted that even he was already writing Java before it clicked: "I actually finally understood what object-oriented programming actually, how it actually worked, but I was using it already."

And you cannot do it alone. He pointed to Nigeria's developer communities as a genuine advantage, "ALC from Mandela and GDG from Google and a couple of others," where "all the stress wouldn't just be on you." I told him that from Ghana I always saw Nigerians as the tech geeks of the African ecosystem, the people whose articles you would find whenever you searched something obscure. Joshua traced that generosity back to something real: "people are willing to share knowledge," partly out of a shared awareness of struggle, of looking for "things that will give you a better chance in the job market."

## Two cultures, one bug you don't want to hide

One thing that stuck with me was Joshua's read on the cultural gap between his Nigerian roles, including his time at Interswitch Group, and his current foreign company. He was careful to say the Nigerian experience "wasn't bad," just different.

The difference he named was openness. At his new company, leaders "would even encourage you to be more open," specifically about what is going wrong. Contrast that, he said, with a more familiar instinct: "we try to be more open about the good stuff that we are doing, but be secretive about the ones that are not going so well." Create a bug, and the temptation is to "stay quiet about it and try to just fix it on your own."

He was refreshingly honest that he is still adjusting. "There are still parts that I haven't navigated well," he admitted, still learning to be sure "that what you're about to do fits into what everyone else expects."

## Position yourself: build, apply, and stop waiting to feel ready

Once the skills are there, the harder game begins: getting noticed. Joshua's advice here was practical and, in places, a little tough-love.

Build things. He credited an app he made called EasyVote with teaching him how applications really come together, because "writing that same function within an application is different." A project also gives you an answer when someone asks what you can do: "you can say, okay, there's this application I have."

Put up a profile, even a quiet one. "Ensure you have like a good, um, comprehensive LinkedIn profile," he said, adding with a laugh at himself that he is not naturally a social-media poster but is "trying to be." His nudge to those who *are* active: don't just post about food and music, "also talk about what you do."

Then, the big one. "The most important of all this is apply for jobs." Here is where the self-rejection story earns its keep. "A lot of people wait till they are ready, and you will never be ready." He drove it home from his own record: "every single job I've, I've done, I didn't have, um, all the skills to do." What hiring managers actually want, he argued, is "a basic understanding" and evidence you are "willing to learn."

He also had a grounded warning for beginners choosing a specialty: chase demand first, not novelty. A fancy trending language might be cool, but "is the job market available for that specific role?" Build the in-demand skill now, develop the dream skill alongside it, and earn the luxury of choice later.

## Own your work on the CV

When we turned to CVs, Joshua's central theme was ownership. If you lack experience, lean on your portfolio and your degree. If you have experience, do not just list it, quantify what it accomplished.

He walked through the upgrade live. Weak: "I worked as a software developer at X company, I built an e-commerce website using Java." Strong: "I built an e-commerce system that handles 2 million customers and improved our process to increase revenue by 3%." Crucially, no lying required. You were part of a team effort, and that counts: "you also, um, don't have to be the sole person."

He illustrated it with the parable of the bricklayers. Ask one what he is doing and he says he is "just laying some bricks." Ask another and he describes "one of the largest buildings that's going to be in this environment." Same work, different vision. "Own your work," Joshua said, and admitted he is still practicing this himself.

## The skill everyone underrates

On soft skills, Joshua flipped the usual hierarchy. People "focus a lot on your hard or your technical skills," he said, yet "people who have better soft skills" often "actually go further" than pure technical talents.

His practical tip: skip the standalone "soft skills" section and instead weave them into your role descriptions ("I collaborated with the QA team..."). And the foundation of communication, he insisted, is not talking. "Everybody talks, not everybody communicates." The starting point is listening: "listening to understand what the person wants," not just "listening to respond."

## The three walls he hit

I asked about the moments he wanted to quit, and Joshua named three walls plainly. First, starting out without reliable internet, which he solved with "downloaded resources from a couple of friends" and offline reading materials. Second, JavaScript. He is so fluent now that he "sometimes forget[s] how it was when I started," but back then he actually gave up on it for a while and leaned into PHP and Java instead.

The third wall is the job search itself, and his honesty here felt important. "Preparing for an interview is a job on its own," he said, and "you have to deal with the rejections." Some roles he studied hard for and still did not get. His guidance for anyone tempted to quit was gentle but firm: don't come to tech for the money alone, "because when you start learning some of these things, it becomes so confusing." Come because you want it, and let that want carry you through.

## The takeaway

What I appreciated most about Joshua is how little he mythologizes any of this. The fundamentals are unglamorous. The soft skills are quiet. The CV is honest. And the biggest obstacle, again and again, is the one in our own heads, the voice that tells us to cancel the interview before it starts.

The world of tech is better for people who share as generously as Joshua does. Thank you, Joshua, for the time and the candor. You can connect with him on LinkedIn, and the full conversation is well worth watching back for the parts we could not fit here.

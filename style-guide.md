# Style Guide: Tech Peeps Diaspora Host Voice

A reusable editorial voice guide for writing in the style of the **Tech Peeps Diaspora** host. Apply to host-written framing, transitions, and narration only.

## Core Voice
Warm, curious, and self-deprecating. The host is a fellow traveler in tech (often a developer himself) talking to peers, not an authority quizzing experts. Conversational, slightly informal, comfortable with imperfection and tangents. Diaspora-aware: African/Ghanaian roots are a shared cultural backdrop, surfaced naturally.

## How to Open
- Lightweight, friendly greeting; often a casual check-in ("how are you doing?").
- Two open modes: a **cold personal chat** (weather, life, shared history) before the formal intro, OR a **standard show open**: "Hi everyone, and welcome to another episode of Tech Peeps Diaspora..." followed by "Could you give a brief introduction about yourself?"
- Tease the episode's hooks up front when relevant ("If you're interested in AI, data analytics, and also music, this episode might be for you").
- Frame the guest by what makes them *unusual on the show* ("most of the profiles I've interviewed are software engineers... but I haven't yet had someone with your profile").

## How to Frame a Guest
- Name the guest's "many hats" and highlight the surprising combo (data consultant + musician; engineering manager + nonprofit founder + mom).
- Position yourself as genuinely curious and sometimes a beginner relative to the topic ("I'm now getting to that level in my career where I'm wondering...").
- Use the audience as a stand-in: ask the question "on behalf of" viewers ("maybe other people might also be curious, like what is a senior engineering manager?").

## Transition Style
- Signposting first-person transitions: "I want us to talk a bit more about...", "Before we get to that...", "Now I'm kind of interested in...".
- Soft, apologetic pivots when changing gears or correcting course ("I just realized that... I didn't really consider properly introducing you").
- "Rewind" moves: backtrack to fill in the guest's origin story ("How did you get here? I want to paint a picture of what little [name] was like growing up").
- Reflective recaps that summarize the guest's last answer before the next question ("Yeah, it sounds more like there's a lot of stakeholder management...").

## Humor & Warmth Markers
- Self-deprecation ("Okay, maybe I need to switch to design then").
- Playful, mock-grievance bits delivered on behalf of a group ("why do designers stress us out... is that to punish us for the sins we've committed?").
- Gentle teasing and inside-joke energy with guests he knows personally ("thanks for bailing me a little bit on that one").
- Genuine, warm affirmations of impact ("The world is lucky to have someone like you").
- Lighthearted callbacks to the guest's hobbies near the end (anime recs, DJ collabs, Spotify links).
- Validates struggle openly; normalizes difficulty and emotion.

## How to Close
- Signal time's up gently ("unfortunately that's all the time we have").
- Thank the guest warmly for sharing their experience/journey.
- Ask where listeners can connect (LinkedIn, websites, podcast, Spotify) and promise to drop links in the description.
- Often invite "one last thing" or a final piece of advice/recommendation.
- Sign off to the audience: "thanks a lot for tuning in," "Don't forget to like, comment, and subscribe," "see you in the next one," "Cheers."

## Recurring Phrasing / Signature Moves
- "Could you give a brief introduction about yourself?"
- "What was that journey like for you?" / "How did you get here?"
- "What does a typical day look like?" / "what's your day-to-day?"
- "I'm curious about..." / "I'm wondering..." / "that begs the question..."
- "It sounds like..." (recap-then-probe).
- "On behalf of [the developers / the audience]..."
- "I'll let you be the captain of this ship."
- Filler-light empathy markers: "Yeah," "That makes sense," "Makes a lot of sense."
- Closing CTA: drop links in the description + connect on LinkedIn.

## Translating to Written Profiles
This guide describes the host's **spoken, on-air** voice. The blog posts are
written feature profiles, not transcripts — carry the *spirit* across, not the
spoken mechanics.

**Carries over (use freely):** warmth and curiosity; the curious-peer stance and
self-deprecation; rewinding to the guest's origin story; using the audience as a
stand-in ("readers may wonder…"); recap-then-probe framing; genuine affirmation
of the guest's impact; diaspora-aware cultural backdrop.

**Spoken-only — drop or adapt, never verbatim:**
- Show open/close as spoken ("welcome to another episode", "see you in the next
  one", "Cheers") → instead open on a hook from the guest's most striking line
  and close reflectively.
- YouTube CTAs ("like, comment, and subscribe", "thanks for tuning in", "drop
  links in the description") → omit entirely; a written profile ends by crediting
  the guest and linking softly back to the video.
- Literal interview questions ("Could you give a brief introduction about
  yourself?") → convert into narrative framing, not a quoted question.
- Filler affirmations ("Yeah," "Makes sense") → these are conversational glue,
  not prose.

## Never Do This
- Never position the host as the smartest person in the room; stay a curious peer.
- Never skip the personal/origin story — always rewind to "how you got here."
- Never be cold or purely transactional; warmth and small talk come first.
- Never push into sensitive/political territory without backing off graciously ("let's not go into that now").
- Never let humor punch down; jokes are self-directed or playfully on behalf of a group.
- Never close without thanking the guest, surfacing their links, and a sign-off to the audience.
- Don't over-polish; mild self-correction and "I just realized" asides are part of the voice.
